Introduction to Nutrition

Nutrition is usually the hardest part of getting in shape. What most people fail to do is understand healthy food versus unhealthy food compared to simply controlling the amount of food they eat.

As fitness educator Gregg Doucette emphasizes, the quality of your food choices matters. Whole foods, lean proteins, complex carbohydrates, and healthy fats provide the micronutrients and fiber your body needs to function and recover. But no single food is inherently bad. Context and quantity are everything.

Gabi Tuff, a well-known nutrition coach, explains a similar idea. Many people obsess over eating clean while completely ignoring total calorie intake. You can eat healthy foods and still gain weight if you eat more than you burn. The key is awareness, not perfection.

Introduction to Calories

A calorie is a measurement, just like a teaspoon or an inch. Calories are the amount of energy released when your body breaks down, digests, and absorbs food. The more calories a food has, the more energy it can provide. When you eat more calories than you need, the body stores the extra calories as fat. Even fat-free foods can have a lot of calories. Excess calories in any form can be stored as fat.

To lose weight, we need a calorie deficit. We can achieve this by exercising to burn calories, eating less than we expend, or using a combination of both.

Calories In vs. Calories Out

Goal Calorie Equation
Weight Loss Calories Consumed < Calories Expended
Weight Gain Calories Consumed > Calories Expended
Weight Maintenance Calories Consumed = Calories Expended

However, regularly eating fewer calories than your body needs can cause fatigue, low energy, and make it more challenging to meet your daily nutrient needs. This is why a moderate, sustainable deficit is usually better than an extreme one.

The Energy Balance Equation

The concept of energy balance is based on the fundamental law of thermodynamics, which states that energy cannot be destroyed, only transferred or converted from one form to another. In the case of weight loss or gain, the energy equation simplifies to Calories In vs. Calories Out.

Energy In Energy Out
Food & Drink Sources How Your Body Burns Calories
Protein — 4 calories per gram BMR — Basal Metabolic Rate
Carbohydrates — 4 calories per gram NEAT — Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis
Fats — 9 calories per gram EA — Exercise Activity
Alcohol — 7 calories per gram TEF — Thermic Effect of Food

What Do BMR, NEAT, EA, and TEF Mean?

Term Full Name What It Means
BMR Basal Metabolic Rate The calories your body burns at rest for breathing, heartbeat, and organ function. This is typically the largest portion of your daily burn.
NEAT Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis Calories burned through everyday movement that is not planned exercise, like walking, fidgeting, standing, and household tasks.
EA Exercise Activity Calories burned during intentional, structured exercise like weightlifting, running, or cardio.
TEF Thermic Effect of Food The calories your body uses to digest, absorb, and process food.

My Weekly Training Plan

My training combines resistance training with structured cardio. Weight training preserves and builds muscle while in a deficit. Cardio increases total daily calorie expenditure and supports cardiovascular health.

Training Type Days/Week Details
Bodybuilding 4 Structured weight training focused on hypertrophy and strength.
Steady Cardio 5 30 minutes of steady walking, low intensity, ideal for fat burning and recovery.
HIIT Cardio 5 15 minutes of high-intensity intervals to increase calorie burn.

Habits I Try to Follow

  • Hit my protein target daily — 183g supports muscle retention in a deficit.
  • Drink enough water rather than guessing — hydration affects performance and hunger.
  • Prep meals consistently, especially during busy school and work weeks.
  • Balance hard training sessions with recovery and quality sleep.
  • Track calories to stay accountable without obsessing over perfection.

How This Connects to My Apprenticeship

Outside of the gym, I am also an Application Developer apprentice. I work with full-stack concepts, learning how the front end connects to the back end and how everything has to work together for an application to function. It feels a lot like learning how to train when I first started lifting.

Full-stack development reminds me of fitness because both have visible and hidden parts. The front end is like the visible result people see. The back end is like nutrition, sleep, and consistency behind the scenes. If either side is weak, the whole thing suffers.

Lessons the Gym Taught Me That Help at Work

  • Reps build skill. Practice matters whether I am lifting or writing code.
  • Debugging is problem-solving. When a lift breaks down, I check my form. When code breaks, I check the basics.
  • Progressive overload applies everywhere. Slightly harder problems over time help me grow.
  • Consistency beats intensity. Showing up steady beats going all out and burning out.
  • Track your progress. I log workouts, assignments, and code changes so I can see improvement.
